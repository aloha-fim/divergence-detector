A new lens on liquidity in U.S. rates.

Quoted spreads describe what a market is willing to do. Executed cost describes what it actually does. When the two disagree, the gap is often where the most useful information lives.

Original feature 8 min read Fixed income · Liquidity

Walk past any rates trading desk and a screen of bid-ask quotes is the first thing you see. Tight spreads, deep books, a thousand markets agreeing to within a thirty-second. The screen is reassuring. It is also, on occasion, almost entirely uninformative.

The reason is simple and well-understood by anyone who has tried to move size in a thin market: a posted quote is firm for a particular trade configuration — a particular size, a particular side, a particular moment. Push beyond those bounds and the screen-priced cost diverges from the executed one. In calm markets the divergence is small enough to ignore. In stressed ones it widens until the screen says one thing and the trade ticket says another. The screen, by then, is no longer measuring liquidity. It is measuring what dealers are willing to quote on light size.

This piece sketches an alternative framing that has been gaining ground in fixed income research: measure liquidity by what gets executed, not by what gets quoted. Then read the gap between the two as its own signal. The result is a coarser but more honest read on whether a market is actually functioning — and a sharper one on when it is about to stop.

What quoted spreads miss.

The bid-ask spread is a beautifully convenient summary. One number per instrument per moment, updated continuously, conceptually obvious. Every desk uses it, every regulator references it, every paper on liquidity opens with it. The problem is what it omits.

It omits depth — how much size sits behind the top quote before the next level. It omits resilience — how fast the book reassembles after a trade prints. It omits session — early-Tokyo cash 30-year is not the same instrument as mid-London cash 30-year. And, most consequentially in stress, it omits the actual cost of executing a benchmark-sized order. A two-tick quote on $5mm tells you almost nothing about what $250mm clears at when the desk is no longer keen to warehouse risk.

This is not a hypothetical. It is the lived experience of every desk that traded the March 2020 dislocation, the September 2022 gilt episode, or the SVB weekend in March 2023. Quoted markets held a respectable face. Execution did not.

Measuring what executes.

If quoted spread is the question we have been answering, the better question is: how much did it cost to do the trade? Measured directly, on prints, normalized so the answer is comparable across instruments and across time.

Industry research — notably Tradeweb's published work on its Liquidity Cost Index — has formalized a version of this idea for U.S. rates. The methodology decomposes the realized transaction cost into components that can be normalized away: trade size (a $250mm clip should not be compared with a $5mm one without adjustment), side (buy-side and sell-side flows trade against different parts of the book), time of day (Asia handover is structurally different from London afternoon), and instrument-specific structure (a CTD futures basis trades differently from a TIPS swap).

The output is a single time series, in basis points, representing what an institutional trade actually cost relative to mid. Aggregated across instruments, it becomes a composite. Aggregated across asset classes, it becomes a regime indicator.

Why does this matter operationally? Because a normalized realized cost is the only liquidity measure that survives the moment when liquidity matters most. Quoted spreads can be — and are — kept artificially narrow by dealers reluctant to widen visibly. They can also widen on light flow as a precaution. Realized cost is the actual print. It does not pretend.

The implied side.

Realized cost answers what is happening. It does not, by itself, answer what the market expects. For that the standard sources are option-implied volatility (MOVE in rates, equivalent surfaces in credit and swaps), risk-reversal skew, and dealer commentary aggregated into sentiment proxies.

These are forward-looking signals. They are also opinions. Implied vol is an opinion of insurers, market-makers, and end-users priced into option chains; sentiment is an opinion summarized from text. Both are useful precisely because they are sometimes wrong — when their disagreement with realized cost is large, it tells you something neither can tell you alone.

Two ways the signals can diverge.

There are two interesting configurations. The first is implied stress with realized calm — option markets are pricing volatility that the cash tape is not validating. This is often, though not always, a fade signal: someone is hedging, vol is expensive relative to what the underlying is doing, and the position resolves with implied compressing rather than realized rising.

The second is realized stress with implied calm — the cash tape is dislocating while options are still priced as if nothing is happening. This is the more dangerous configuration. The execution is real; the implied side has not caught up. Tomorrow's repricing is in front of you, not behind.

The asymmetry.

Place implied stress on one axis and realized stress on the other and you get four quadrants. They are not equal in importance. Two of them — the diagonal — are the markets agreeing with themselves. The other two, where the axes disagree, are where the most actionable information lives.

Execution stressed with implied calm is historically the dangerous configuration. Not the reverse. The central observation

Why is the dislocated quadrant — implied low, realized high — the more informative one? Two reasons. First, the implied side is generally faster than dealer pricing to react to news, so its silence in the face of stressed execution implies the stress is microstructural rather than headline-driven. That tends to mean liquidity provision has thinned in a specific corner of the market that broader signals are missing. Second, when implied vol does catch up — and historically, it does — the repricing is larger because it is starting from a lower base.

Reading the historical record.

The clearest examples are the ones every fixed-income trader can recite. March 2020: cash Treasury markets dislocated days before MOVE caught up, with on-the-run / off-the-run basis blowing out while option-implied vol still looked relatively contained on a one-week basis. The dislocation quadrant was occupied first; the agreement quadrant followed.

March 2023, the SVB weekend: the rates flight happened in cash before option markets repriced. Realized T-cost in 2-year Treasuries went vertical on the Friday before the weekend; MOVE moved on the Monday. Again, dislocation preceded agreement.

September 2022, the UK gilt episode: a fast-moving, headline-driven crisis where for once implied moved with the cash market rather than after it — but the cross-asset breadth (USD swap spreads, off-shore funding) is what carried the contagion signal that gilts alone could not.

Operational uses.

What is the discipline actually for? Three applications in particular justify the modeling work.

01

Pre-trade risk

Before sizing an order, knowing whether the instrument is in dislocation vs. agreement quadrant changes the prudent block size and the prudent venue.

02

Post-trade analysis

TCA against quoted spread overstates execution skill in calm regimes and understates it in stressed ones. Normalizing against realized-cost percentile is fairer.

03

Cross-asset surveillance

Dislocation in one instrument is interesting. Dislocation across three asset classes simultaneously is the regime call.

What the discipline does not give you.

It does not predict direction. A dislocated MBS basis tells you the mortgage market is executing under strain; it does not tell you which way prepays or rates resolve from here. It does not eliminate judgment. The 2x2 matrix is a useful frame for organizing reactions, not a replacement for them. And it does not produce a single number that summarizes liquidity in one figure — partly because no single number could honestly do that, partly because the work of a dashboard like the one this article accompanies is to give you several numbers and let you weigh them.

Closing.

None of this is a critique of bid-ask spread as a measure. It does what it does — it summarizes the top of book at a moment in time — and there is real engineering elegance in how compact a thing it is. The point is narrower: the moments when bid-ask spread is most reassuring are sometimes the moments it is least informative. A market that quotes calmly while executing under strain has not solved the strain. It has merely declined to show it on the screen.

For most sessions, none of this matters. The realized and implied sides agree, the divergence is small, and the conventional measures are fine. The cases the discipline is built for are the half-dozen sessions a year where it does matter — and those are typically the ones a desk most wants to have a clear read on.
