Measuring what matters: a new lens on liquidity in U.S. rates.

In institutional rates markets, what gets executed can diverge sharply from what gets implied. A new index from Tradeweb measures liquidity in the units traders actually use—basis points and ticks, across products, across cycles.

Bhas Nalabothula Head of U.S. Institutional Rates, Tradeweb
Sean Hu Head of U.S. Institutional Rates Data Science, Tradeweb
March 24, 2026 6 min read
Data analysis Mihir Chinai · Steve Qiu · Gabriel Zanuttini-Frank · Rene Ulloa  —  U.S. Institutional Rates Data Science

Few concepts are invoked as often in fixed-income markets as liquidity—and few are measured as poorly. In U.S. institutional rates, where daily turnover runs into the trillions, the quality of liquidity governs not only the cost of execution but how quickly the market processes new information and recalibrates pricing.

Conventional gauges—posted bid-ask spreads, option-implied volatility, dealer color—each tell part of the story. Each is also, by construction, a measure of what the market expects or what it is showing on a screen at one moment. None of them tells you what it cost to actually move size.

That gap—between what the market projects and what it delivers—widens fastest under stress. The week following "Liberation Day" in April 2025 made the point sharply. Equities sold off more than 10.5%; the VIX printed above 65, a reading previously seen only during the 2008 financial crisis and the March 2020 pandemic. And yet realized transaction cost in rates was meaningfully less strained than during either of those earlier episodes. By the implied measures, this was a crisis. By execution, it was a difficult week.

Without a consistent way to put those episodes alongside each other, the contrast was easy to miss in real time. What the market needed was a measure of liquidity rooted not in what was being implied, but in what was actually being executed.

From implied signals to realized conditions.

The Tradeweb Liquidity Cost Index addresses that gap. Built on aggregated transaction-level data drawn from the firm's institutional rates platform, it captures the realized cost of executing benchmark trades and the actual intraday price movement of U.S. interest-rate products. The inputs are observed prints—not models, not forward curves, not opinions.

T-Cost sits at the center of the framework. Expressed in basis points—the unit a trader uses on every ticket—it captures the realized cost of trading benchmark size during principal market hours. The question it answers is the one that matters when sizing a trade: what did this market actually cost today, relative to what it has cost historically?

When markets are agitated, the feel and the function can diverge sharply. The execution data discriminates between liquidity that has genuinely degraded and liquidity that has merely been repriced. That is a useful distinction—often the more useful one.

Three measures, four asset classes.

T-Cost is the centerpiece, but it does not stand alone. The framework adds two complementary measures: a Volatility Index that captures realized intraday price movement normalized to basis points per day, and a Composite Width series that tracks how tightly Tradeweb's composite pricing is quoted at any moment. Volatility reflects what the market actually moved—not what option chains thought it would. Composite width reflects how wide a dealer book is willing to stand on size.

One framework, one set of units, four very different parts of the curve. That consistency is the architectural point. Comparing execution conditions across products, and across time, requires that they be measured in the same way.

Context across market cycles.

The longest tail of the dataset is what makes the framework genuinely useful. With several years of history, today's reading can be evaluated against the distribution of prior conditions—not against intuition, not against the last analogous episode someone happened to remember, but against the full record.

That historical anchoring matters because the same absolute T-Cost can be interpreted very differently in different contexts. A reading at the 95th percentile of the past year and a reading at the 50th percentile of a longer window are not the same signal. The framework lets a portfolio manager or risk officer say with precision: this is what a stressed session has historically looked like, and this is where today sits inside it.

By the implied measures, this was a crisis. By execution, it was a difficult week. On Liberation Day, April 2025

The framework also surfaces something harder to see in any single instrument: cross-asset divergence. Liquidity does not move uniformly. Sessions in which one corner of the rates complex dislocates while others remain orderly are common—and they carry information that single-instrument measures cannot.

The clearest recent example came on January 9, 2026. Following an administration order for Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac to purchase $200 billion in mortgage-backed securities, execution costs in MBS spiked. T-Cost in the agency MBS index printed roughly 500% above its one-year median. Treasury T-Cost, on the same day, remained stable—just below its own one-year median. MBS tightened more than 20 basis points against Treasuries as the market priced in the demand shock.

Side-by-side, the divergence was immediate and quantifiable. Without a consistent cross-asset frame, it would have been visible only to participants who already knew where to look.

A more practical benchmark.

The framework is built for use, not for display. Execution strategies depend on knowing what trades are actually costing. Risk management depends on distinguishing between elevated implied stress and elevated realized stress—they imply different positioning. Performance benchmarking depends on a reference point that reflects what the market delivered, not what it might have implied under different circumstances.

Grounding the indices in observed transaction data and expressing them in basis points (or 32nds, for MBS) gives every desk a common vocabulary. A 0.79 print in MBS T-Cost on January 9 is not an abstract z-score. It is an executable number that traders can read, compare, and act on—immediately.

The roadmap from here is the obvious one: broader coverage, finer time resolution, and intraday live series. As liquidity infrastructure evolves, the lens has to evolve with it.

Liquidity, in the end, is hard to define in words. Measured in basis points and ticks—consistently, historically, and across products—it becomes something else: a quantity that traders, risk officers, and portfolio managers can reason about as precisely as price itself.
